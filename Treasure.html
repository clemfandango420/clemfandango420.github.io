Bandits covers "normal" hoards and those in war-torn areas. Bandits I is a local camp, Bandits III is a bandit-king.
Tombs are historical sites and ruins. Tomb I is a local ruin - Tomb V is the burial site of a historical conqueror.
Temples are both active and ruined holy sites. Temple I is a regional temple - Temple III is the central temple of a religion.
Monstrous covers hoards by those monsters known to accumulate treasure. Monstrous I is a goblin nest - Monstrous V is the wealth of a demon.
Magic-User covers hoards generated by those pursuing magical arts. Magic User I is a small hedge wizard - Magic User IV is a lich or wizard-king.
A Mythic hoard is ancient treasure, dragon hoards etc - the campaign defining haul. Items will be hidden beneath mountains of conventional wealth.

20%:1 means a 20% chance of single item of that type being present.

Coins

Hoards of coins do not occur under normal conditions. Coins are hoarded as things begin falling apart. Hoards which are found were never claimed by their originator.

Coins taken from pockets with grubby fingers will be of mixed modern types, and usable as tender unless a polity is actively enforcing its own coinage.

Coins discovered in hoards will tie to the time of their origin - use the below Special Coinages if uncertain.
Certain accumulative hoards as gathered by certain types of inhuman monsters (ogres, bankers etc) will mostly be of local, modern types (as dictated by geography*) but may (20% chance) have one of the below Special Coins. Special Coins can be used as normal coinage or sold to historians, scholars and collectors.

*n.b. viking hoards reveal silver dhirrams alongside less distant coinage.

1. Bronze Imitations

Prior to the near-extinction of local indigenous culture, they lived alongside the eventual conqueror-exterminators. They would cast imitation coinage in bronze - not in deceit, but aspirationally. The faces of the fractious chiefs and druids replace the lineages of emperors. The shape of writing rendered as a pattern, robbed of meaning. Eventually, once fortunes reversed, these bronze imitations became the coin of the realm, the erstwhile conquerors melting down their golden discs.

Thick, crude coins. Each worth 1sp, 20sp to a collector.
Found in groups of 5d20 amongst hoards in the lands of the former empire.
Obverse - Druids, Horned Helms, Battleaxes, Rampant Horses.
Reverse - Ships, Roads, Trees, Near-Writing.

2. Silver Tetradrachm

Silver beaten to the thinnest possible edge whilst retaining any integrity - they can easily be folded into nuggets. Issued by an early merchant city to combat rampant clipping - any attempted modification utterly destroying the coinage. If suspended and struck, they chime like bells - a request, apocryphally, from the husband of a particularly successful merchant who insisted on counting their collection personally.

Razor-thin silver coins. Each worth 1sp, 10sp to a collector. Worthless to collectors if folded.
Found in stacks of 2d100 amongst hoards in historical trading zones.
Obverse & Reverse - no markings

3. Coat of Coins

Barbarian kings commissioned these mail-coin-coats as a sign of wealth and scorn for the unpractical wealth of civilised people. These coats are found near-exclusively as grave-goods of such kings - and many still honour them. Wearing such a coat is sure to alert those remaining faithful to your sacrilege.

The most successful kings had patterns formed of coins of many subjugated kingdoms - the complementary shapes increasing the protection afforded, as well as flaunting victory over more subject peoples.

Provides AC as Leather if a single coinage is used, AC as Chain if 2-3 coinages are used, and AC as Plate if 4+ coinages are used.

1d10*1000 Heterogeneous coins sewn to backing fabric. Random coins can be pulled off at 1sp each, or the coat sold for 1000sp to a collector.
Found as a singleton only.
Vanishingly rare. Use other coins to determine obverse & reverse.

4. Victory Aureus

Each was minted for a year in celebration of a Society General defeating another foreign kingdom. The coins always depict a wolf striking down a feeble animal - the national animal of a defeated people. Beyond this heavy-handed iconography, there is no further mention of victory upon the coins. Some are mentioned only via the coins - they were too minor to be included in their annals.

Chunky electrum coins. Worth 10sp, 20sp to a collector.
Found in stashed hoards and barbarian hauls, 1d6*100.
Obverse - Side-profile heads & date markings
Reverse - Wolf striking down animals

5. Doubleface Drachm

Silver coins marked by two oddities - the obverse always depicts a pair of faces, each facing outwards albeit tilted away. There seems to be endless variations - implying hand-made coins or a very long run. The second oddity is on the reverse - small lines and markings implying an order but without any obvious guide. The obverse and reverse are always paired. The originating city is unknown.

If 500 unique coins can be assembled (1% chance of any two given coins being the same) and arranged, the reverse is revealed to be a map. The centre depicts a city found on no other map. You would not be the first to try and assemble the map.

Heavy silver coins. Worth 15sp, 30sp to a casual collector, 100sp to someone assembling the map.
1d6 found in many hoards - as if a curio.
Obverse - A pair of faces.
Reverse - Disorganised lines (map sections to the initiated).

6. Twisted Javelinheads

Prior to coinage being widespread, trade across the archipelago was facilitated through the trade of these bronze javelinheads - the tin used requiring significant effort to import from distant isles. In legends, kings would make scaled armour by hammering them flat and riveting them together. Now they can be found, coated in verdigris across the arid land.

Bronze pointed cone. Worth 5sp, 25sp to a collector.
Found near the oldest cities and those who raided them in [1. 1d6, singletons 2. 1d20, stacked 3.1d100, loose 4. d6*100, stacked and tied]

7. Overclipped Coins

Amongst criminal classes, badly or overly clipped coins are still exchanged at half-value. They tend to accumulate into ever-larger hoards in the interior, but are easily passed as legitimate on the borderlands - ensuring their dispersal. Some are surprised when they find the genuine article, their smaller kingdoms having adopted these clipped coins as their own specie.

Various, but thin, cracked, damaged, sharp or rough. Worth half conventional value. Worth nothing to a collector.
Constitutes 30% of a hoard near a current or previous border. Criminal stashes have 1d20*100 coins.
Obverse & Reverse - barely distinguishable.

8. Gold Imitations

The story goes that a barbarous kingdom tried to ape an empire by casting their own imitations - but being far richer in gold than silver, they created coins of much greater value than intended.
This story is a lie - they were attempting to sway mercantile interest as a bulwark against the empire. Instead, they summoned armies.

Chunky gold coins - worth 10sp, 50sp to a collector.
Found in the ruins of occupier military camps and sunken mercantile vessels, 4d20 at a time.
Obverse - Side-profile heads and dates. Reverse - Horses, Wolves, Roads, Lighthouses.

9. Nomad Bowls

From the steppe they road and burnt cities who would not bend the knee. Rather than quibble over coins and exchange, the conquerors established something new. A small thimble with a matching rod. Other coins are socketed into the thimble, and pounded into a stackable bowl with the rod.
Those found using any other value-token were strapped to the back of a horse and dragged a mile for each illegal coin used.
Gifting the thimble and rod was a mark of great honour - or of collaboration with the occupiers.

Small bowls of gold and silver - worth 10/5sp respectively, worth 30/20sp to a collector. Found in stacks of 2d100 silver and 1d100 gold in hoards everywhere - traded widely.

A thimble-and-rod would be worth 1500sp to a collector.

10. Cowry Shells

The delicate curves, soft cream shade and toothy opening uniquely identify these shells, used for trade across borders since time immemorial. Far lighter than coins, many more can be carried - and far harder to falsify. Across the weight of history, far more blood has been shed over these little shells than coins of gold and silver.

Small shell - worth 2sp in various cultures and all coastal cities. Has no collectors. Found in wrecks and near the coast in sacks of 2d8*100 or 1d6 "threads" of 100 each.

11. Dog Spirit Bank

A ward against thieves and idle spending - coins stashed away within these fired pottery dogs. Fat, squat and heavy, their collars are marked with protection glyphs. They vary in size from the palm of your hand to life-size - and each is stamped with numerals atop their head. Breaking one was taboo - so as they filled up, they became the unit of exchange themselves.
The story says that those who tried to fill their dog-banks with lead weight would die at night, bellies full of sharpened coins.

Pottery Dog - worth 50, 100, 250 or 500sp to a collector. Can be smashed for half this value in small silver coins which can be spend normally. 10% chance of still containing a guardian spirit. Can do anything a dog or ghost could do - will haunt the breaker until a new dog-pot is made and filled.
Found in buried estates and as grave-goods. Peasants would have a 50sp dog, and nobles 2d6 500sp dogs.
Obverse (of the coins) - Dogs, Roads, Trees. Reverse (of the coins) - Depictions of internal organs.

12. Demonface Coins

Overwrought, heavy, and unable to stack - these coins are ill-suited by most measures. They are not even made of a precious metal - they consist of pitted iron. Each depicts a snarling demonic face, which projects outwards from the coinface.
When conjured up and questioned, demons themselves are only quiet and uncomfortable when it comes to these coins. They are bound to not speak on them.
The coins drink heat hungrily, but do not let it go - they are always cold to the touch.
If broken, the demon bound within is released, and must perform one act for the breaker.

Iron sculpture-coins - worth 2000sp to a very particular type of collector.
Found singleton in particularly ancient or sorcerous hoards.
Obverse - Completely unique snarling demon face. Reverse - Detailed circles and protective glyphs.

Artefacts (d20)

Where artefacts are present, roll or select from the below.

1. Drowning Weights

To be married to the Sea is considered unseemly to these coastal people. The sea is like an animal, to be tamed and used, not loved.

Found in hidden, shameful places, these are dinner-plate sized iron disks with square holes in the centre. They are usually an inch thick, with no decorations or designs.

Before adulthood is obtained, one is expected to wear the weights and reject the Sea's embrace. Those lost, it is hoped, sate the hungry waters. They act as a reminder for those who return.

The genuine, used articles are worth 250sp to collectors, removed enough to not fully grasp what such an item being for sale represents.

Those openly carrying them on the coast are shunned. They might wake up, pinned down and branded.

2. Treeheart

Foresters will speak of heartwood - the central core of the tree, shrouded in sapwood.
This knot of smooth, unpolished wood is not that.
Children lost to the wood sometimes return, clutching one of these fist-sized nodules. As long as they retain the nodule, they have nothing to fear from the donor forest.
This charm does not follow possession of the bauble, although stories claim it does. Second-hand possessors will find themselves wandering the woods in dreams. Something follows them.

Worth 500sp to collectors.

3. Faerie Boats

Tiny bronze boats, washed up on the riverbank. Before a maiden voyage, they are cast out into the waters - either to trick the river, satiate it or thank it, depending who is asked. Removing one from the river is said to doom the vessel.

During petty wars and raids, youths known for prodigious patience and lung capacity are set to find and steal such charms. Many have died in the river, pierced by guardians or dragged away elsewhere by the flow, bodies never found. The grimmest stories have them as crew to these tiny bronze boats.

Worth 150sp to collectors.

4. Cloak of Hands

Named for the palm-shaped leaves used to construct it, these cloaks are prized for their low weight and waterproof nature. As long as they are kept watered and exposed to sunlight, the leaves are capable of surviving indefinitely. They also function as camouflage - something relied upon by bandits and resistance fighters. Soldiers of many nations have charged into the undergrowth to find nothing.

If the wearer is struck by a single slashing attack, the cloak is ruined.

Worth 25sp natively, 200sp beyond the borders.

5. Old Key

When property must be abandoned there is much to consider. Many plan on returning, and keep a key. They are passed down generations, against the day of their return. Every key is an unfulfilled promise, a rusted chain of custody. Their tales cipher maps and directions to the promised places, even if young ears and old tongues do not recognise them as such.
If stolen, pursuit is inevitable.

Worth 5sp.

6. God Casket

Iron-bands coil around a box, wood soaked in bitumen. It was not built to be opened, only closed. Within is the god of a city, long buried, the inhabitants moved on by time. When the first to ride carts for war came, they would conquer. As their gore-splattered vehicles moved through the streets, the royal guard would storm the temple and steal the city-god - the ultimate sign of defeat. Such prizes were sealed away in these boxes - to demoralise and control the now-captive populace. Even when their victory was revisited upon them, the gods were not liberated.
How they were scattered is not known.

Worth 350sp, or 5000sp to one seeking to reunite a people.

7. Falcon Hood

The Watchful bid to blind the hunting birds roosting in their temple - each hood a facsimile of one of the Hundred Faces Used to Watch. Each bird loses it's name, becomes the current incarnation of the lineage. The birds originated many of the current hunting lines - the names of such ciphers for the names of each of the Hundred Faces Used to Watch.

Worth 350sp to a collector.

8. Copied Codex

Across mountains, seas and bogs they came, dying as they did. Whatever they brought must be important. Words without interpreters - those died along the way - given with ceremony and reverence. So copies were made. To die in service of such an item clarifies its importance.

This is six or seven generations removed, and the original words are no longer legible - rendered only as decorative snarls of lines and elegant swoops. They are still treated with reverence.

When the source church discoverers them, they are burnt as heretical. Bands of believers have ambushed such imposters already.

Worth 500sp to the church - as a bounty.

9. Crypt Censer

As the dead lie unmoving, scented smoke snakes through the corridors, filling the air - pressing in upon any who stir from rest. As long as the air is filled, peace reigns in the crypt.
The delicate silver walls of the censer are stained nicotine-yellow from the low, slow burn. Some herbs remain within. It remains warm to the touch, despite present circumstance.

Somewhere, a crypt is full of restless corpses.

Worth 350sp to a collector. If openly sold, the party are marked as grave-robbers.

10. Mummified Dog

The acid waters of the bog would have delivered this animal to eternity - but instead it has been vomited up, boneless-yet-rigid, tanned by slow liquid seep. It was promised an eternal hunting ground.

You could put it on a bookshelf.

Worth 150sp to a collector.

11. Gilt Chariot

An oversized, heavy construction of wood, trimmed in gold, wheels as tall as a man and shod in iron. The double-crossbar yoke extending forward fits eight horses. Hooks for war-trophies stud much of the structure. No expense was spared. The sides of the chariot are decorated with paper-mache scenes of carnage - huge figures tear down walls, eat men whole, burn forests.

Worth 3000sp to a collector.

12. Brand

A broken ring, radiating spikes. Heated in a fire until cherry-red, then applied to the cheek of an oathbreaker.
Healers displaced and outcast would say the only cure for branding was burying the tool used to apply it.

Worth nothing - they are still made today.

13. Ivory Miniature

Fits in the hand comfortably, the cream-yellow of the ivory drinking in light. The figure is squared, each face depicting a different figure. The first is a child, a tree leaning over them. The next is an adult, holding tools. The third clutches a child. The fourth face has been abraded smooth. Time has devoured the final face. Suggestions are all that remain - enough to justify any interpretation.

Worth 200sp to a collector.

14. Stone Sword

A uselessly blunt stone sword - barely functional as a club. Blood splatters have been carved into the blade, simulating use. The stone is light and porous.

Worth 100sp to a collector. In 1d20 weeks, they may try and contact you again.

The sword does not stand alone. In a bog, thick with fog, a statue of a cyclops clutches it's eye. A wound has been sculpted in the eye - which perfectly fits the sword. Completed, it reveals a tomb, wherein giants were interred to deny them rest. The old stories claim the heroes guard the corpses against awakenings.

15. Genealogical Tree

Cast in gold, silver and electrum, this figure of a tree stands a foot high, delicate leaves each printed with a name. The branches and leaves trace the lineage of a kingdom abandoned - the villages, towns, people still present but the animus of a royal family entirely absent. In the base of the figure, blank leaves and inscription tools.

Worth 3500sp to potential monarchs.
Worth killing you over to stay free of kings.

16. Fairypenny

An inflexible, leathery disk - one face with the distinctive gills of a mushroom. Pleasant to turn over and over in your fingers. Dirty like an old coin. Only found in old collections, in basements and tombs. The origin is lost - perhaps from over-harvesting.

Worth 50sp to a collector - 250sp in goods in rural communities.

A mark of good fortune, and a gentle warning - the bearer is not to be cheated nor wronged. The punishments vary from spoiled milk to stolen children.

17. Puddingstone

Atop a witches coffin, or worn around the neck - the puddingstone is held to be a protective charm. Each puddingstone is shaped, the many smaller pebbles polished to stand against the background material. The amulets are rarely found - the slabs used to seal coffins are far more common.

Worth 150sp to a collector.

Removing a puddingstone from a grave is a cause for open exile. If any witches remain, they are likely to seek you out and thank you.

18. Sun Amulet

Dirt-encrusted gold - wide as a dinnerplate, with a chain to match. The sun smiles beatifically. Long has it craved the touch of light. They were scattered and buried when the invaders came. Those who buried them were killed for hiding wealth, and so the amulets stayed lost.

Worth 250sp to a collector. Worth 500sp to the remaining Sun-Cultists.

The Sun-Cult will find you if this discovery is public. They will beg you to show them where. If you do not comply, they will try and slander you - they are used to working in the shadows.

19. Fool's Cymbals

The blood of siblings is not spilled lightly in the courts to the West - other punishments are devised for those that plot and scheme. Cymbals and bells, chained and locked to the body. Removal would take hours, and carries a taboo.

Worth 500sp to a collector.

The sound is now associated with fratricide - the indignity accelerated bloodshed rather than cooling tempers. The cymbal is forbidden in courts and halls that remember.

20. Ship's Head

A ship's spirit can be broken without sinking. Boarded, one who used to be a woodsman, far removed from home, moves to the prow of the vessel and strikes off her head. This prize is carried back, leaving the victim unfit for sea in the eyes of superstitious sailors. If the head is buried ashore, even worse - the ship is doomed to sink.

Worth 350sp to collector.

Magic Items (d100)

01-10. Magic Weapons (d20)

1. Stone Knife

Found in the soil. Blunt, rounded. Has no guard - a loose grip ensures cut fingers. Carved into each is a face, mouth yawning open.
Drinks blood. Note damage dealt. This blood can be wrung out and drunk - healing HP equal to damage dealt.
Stores up to 15HP.

Deals 1d6-1 Damage.

The face is that of an Eater - a class of spirit in the local folkloric traditions. Whether the knife represents, contains, or is an Eater is unclear. Wielding it openly results in protective gestures, narrowed eyes and terse conversation. At night, the brave or desperate might steal into your camp to steal the knife.

2. Javelin of Thorns

The bones of something unnamed and forgotten, washed up on a shore. Not a wholeness, but a collection of many. Tiny bone splinters align to a single point, and quiver in the dry. They miss the embrace of wet flesh. Those pierced by this javelin are infested with these thorns, the javelin burrowing and unfolding, deep into their flesh. It cannot be removed without butchery. This takes a Turn.

Deals 1d6 hits of 1d6 damage each. Any surviving this blow take -5 to all rolls, every movement agony.
If a throw misses, 2-in-6 chance the javelin is destroyed.
If used in melee, deals 1d6 damage to the wielder.

Useless against those without living flesh.

The wielder of the Javelin will find themselves challenged to feats of accuracy by all who recognise this weapon.

3. Revenant Sword

A warrior must be armed in the afterlife. To achieve this, their blade would be broken, and cast into the river nearest their birth-hall.
Fish out these pieces, and recast them at night, adding lead. This disarms the spirit, and infuses the blade with fury. It is always cold to the touch.

Deals 1d6+2 damage. If a 6 is rolled, roll 1d6. On a 6, the blade shatters, eager to fail.

Flaunting such a weapon sours relationships. None would move against you directly, but doors are sure to close. Rumours spread of necromancers and black dogs.

4. Eyebiter

Shaft of elm and head of silver. The head is serrated, and gleams like the eyes of shark. Eyebiter has split a hundred helmets - she delights in the wet warmth within the head, hidden behind the eye. Other flesh spoils her appetite. Buried with a wielder far less famous than the spear itself - enough to have been considered lost.

Deals 1d6 damage. Adds 2 to hit and damage rolls versus humans. Subtracts 2 to all other hit and damage rolls.

Eyebiter's reputation is conferred upon the wielder - a killer of men.

5. Drowned-Dog

Dangerous animals are not always terminated - some are kept nearby, under chains and muzzles, to be set loose amongst enemies.
A haft of ash-grey, leading into a waxed leather sack. It sloshes when moved, and smells of salt. The seawater within keeps the axe-head asleep. If removed, the axe springs to life, teeth of flame snarling over the cutting edge of the blade. It jumps like an ill-trained hound, too keen on the hunt. To not attack a moving thing every round a Strength roll is required. Likewise, forcing it back into the bag requires such a feat. Failure in either instance causes 1d6 damage to the wielder.

Deals 1d6+3 damage. Adds 1 to hit.

Those who are thralls to weapons are not welcome in peaceful places. They are tolerated where they might be useful.

6. Giantish Sword

Too big. The hands of captives worked the smaller details into the blade, the guard, the handle. Between scenes of captivity are pleas for release, longings for death - too fine for giant eyes to see. Such miserable prayers only accumulate power as they are denied.

Deals 2d6 damage. Subtracts 2 to hit. Cannot be swung by a mortal - unless swung at one who denies others freedom.

Such an immense weapon ensures attention from all quarters. Any who recognise it for a Giant blade will urge you to bury it, fearing a return.

7. Bloodball

Cast from an admixture of serpents blood and powdered rams skulls, these pinkish nodules delight in the killing of kings and guardians. The mould to make more was destroyed in the final invasion of the isles. There are less than 500 left.

Launched by a sling, deals 1d6+2 damage to those in authority over the launcher. Otherwise, they are harmless.

3d12 are found at a time.

All have demands upon a liberator who does not hide. Kings command surrender, the downtrodden plead liberation.

8. Proven Sons

whereupon riddled,
made a bed for war-thorn
mound-hunters fate once more upon the earth.
and earls and trusted men
crept forward and from forge-bat
plucked a dozen proven sons.

Shot from a bow, deals 1d6+6 damage against Dragons and others of their ilk. Otherwise, treat as a normal arrow.

Single arrows are found occasionally.

9. Partsword

An unornamented knife belies a hidden blade, hammered to an edge between the flickering of the sun and invisible to the human eye. It needs a full sized scabbard - your murderous intent must still be signalled with knife-grip for the surprise to have full effect.

76-80. Magic Grimoires (d10)

Magical grimoires reward repeated study. Each has a difficulty score. This is how many weeks of study it requires before a spell is wrested from the grimoire. If your game is slower, this is weeks of intensive study. Pulpier games may allow study at camp to count.

1. Firelight Bead

An amber bead with murky forms contained within. If held in the right position before a fire, pictograms are projected. They depict hunters, animals, fire, shelter and weather patterns, the movement of the flames animating them. They tell stories, if you allow yourself to listen.

Difficulty 2.
Spells: Animal Growth, Charm Animal, Control Weather, Hallucinatory Terrain, Lightning Bolt, Speak with Animals, Turn Undead.

2. Child of Stone

Within the earth things grow. The earth does not love all of them equally. Some are banished.
Upon the lava plains they can be found - an armful of stone, egg-shaped, the rejected child of stone. Without the warm embrace of the earth, they dream, hoping to escape their isolation.
Bathed in molten lead, they awaken, and can be queried. They can act as tutors and sages - but all their knowledge is bitter.

Difficulty 1.
Spells: Animate Dead, Baleful Polymorph, Curse, Circle of Protection(The Elements), Cloudkill, Fireball, Move Earth, Stone to Flesh.

3. Learned Spider

Whereupon arachnid minds learnt to weave spells is unknown - but this is a member of that secretive, aloof order. It is willing to share, encoding incantations in legible forms within webs. Why it is willing to do so is far less scrutable.

Difficulty 3.
Spells: Cure Light Wounds, Bless, Hold Monster, Locate Object, Speak with Dead, Alter Self, Charm Person, Detect Magic, Haste.

4. Rat King Nerve-Map

A Rat-King beneath a tower, within a dungeon, crawled up from some underworld - the origin is immaterial. The processing reveals the secret - flesh rendered away, a dozen squeaking voices pleading in every known tongue, until only the nerves are left. They are laminated, over and over, spread over a board, interlinked - and such delicate connections reveal sympathetic vibrations in the nature of things.

Difficulty 4.
Spells: Animal Growth, Detect Invisibility, Invisibility, Sleep, Circle of Protection (Domesticated Animals), Lightning Bolt, Stone to Flesh.

5. Sevenfold Path Beyond the Gate

A series of eight near-transparent vellum scrolls. All reference on another extensively - particularly the first. This acts as the gate. The opening acts as a cipher for all the rest - without enclosure, the space is rendered infinite and impossible. Only by passing and re-passing through the gate is the reader/practitioner able to circumvent the need for the gate.

Difficulty 7.
Spells: Anti-Magic Shield, Clairvoyance, Comprehend Languages, Detect Invisibility, Detect Magic, Dispel Magic, Dimension Door, Disintegrate, Fog Wall, Hallucinatory Terrain, Passwall, Polymorph, Teleport.

6. Confessions & Extractions

A small tome with a large lock. The surface has some minimal warping. If the lock is destroyed or a key found, the contents are revealed - the results of torture and witch-trails. The questions reveal a fascination by the torturer - but no mercy. As they converged on understanding, the answers become more usable. What the author did not realise - some of these spells are traps.

Difficulty 4 or Difficulty 1 if reader forgoes the ability to sleep due to nightmares.
Spells: Animate Dead, Baleful Polymorph, Bless/Curse, Charm Person, Colour Spray, Control Water, Control Weather, Cure Light, Serious, Critical Wounds, Enervate Dead, Haste, Invisibility, Locate Object, Plant Growth, Sleep.

7. Library Flagstone

A large stone, the top appearing slightly molten and very smooth. A flagstone from a library in a time and place where magic was learnt openly. Such a structure was inevitably burnt. The flagstone saw generations of learning - and some lessons well enough to repeat. Sleep upon it as a pillow, and resonate.

Difficulty 3, but does not require study.
Spells: Anti-Magic Shield, Circle of Protection, Clairvoyance, Cloudkill, Comprehend Languages, Detect Invisibility, Detect Magic, Dispel Magic, Fly, Haste, Invisibility, Knock, Light, Locate Object, Phantasm, Sleep, True Seeing, Web, Witch-Lock.

8. Twelfth-Puzzle

A tangle of silver and gold shapes, all moving independently yet held together in some unified organising principle. A toy, a puzzle. As one learns to manipulate it, they find themselves drawn inwards. It has lessons to teach for those able to solve it.

Difficulty 4.
Spells: Charm Person, Colour Spray, Confusion, Fear, Haste, Move Earth, Neutralize Poison, Sixth Sense, Slow, Water Breathing.

9. Blood of the Magi

A syringe, glass clear and metalwork brass. Blood, impossibly liquid, sloshes within. Injecting it hurts - years rush into your veins. The blood takes time to adjust. It slowly reaches the brain, and learns to talk. It is the essence of Deemethresu, a magi removed for generations. They will teach you. They are inescapable. They will only allow themselves to dissipate once all their knowledge has been passed on.

Difficulty 1.
Spells: Baleful Polymorph, Charm Monster, Colour Spray, Confusion, Comprehend Languages, Continous Light, Dispel Magic, Fear, Fireball, Fly, Haste, Hold Monster, Invisibility, Light, Project Image, Slaying Spell, Wall of Fire, Wall of Ice, Witch Eye.
Ego 5 (see Staves)

10. Om

A huge assemblage of percussive instruments in a space designed specifically for their playing can, under the right conditions, produce the Sound. The Sound is resonant with Creation. One standing in the right spot to fully experience the Sound would gain some sort of Enlightenment. A magical aspirant would gain that knowledge. The Sound alters the instruments and the space such that it cannot be reproduced by them ever again.

Difficulty 0.
Spells: All.

Through dentistry, one could try and force one into their mouth, needing to remove 1d4 teeth to make space. Human gums are too eager to accept these visitors - healing only takes a week, spent spitting blood. Each tooth has a random effect, usable once a day.

d8 Effect

2 Spit a venom which causes bloody vomiting for several days before recovery. A mouthful is enough to taint a village well.

One with these teeth in their mouth is sure to be burnt as a witch, if discovered. Despite this, worth 2500sp each to jaded nobles and furtive practitioners.

2. Screaming Head

Cleft from the shoulders of the last of the Witch-men, this head still screams, eyes filled with malice, teeth sharp. They were driven into the woods, their homes burnt. The head will not die whilst the last generations of Witch-men keep the body alive in their subterranean lairs - living tombs for an extinguished people.

If the gag is removed, the ear-splitting screech forces all in earshot to Save or be deafened (for 1d6 Turns) and stunned (for 1d4 rounds). The Witch-men can always hear this scream, no matter the distance. A hunting band pursues.

The name Witch-men has more to do with assumptions of the beholder than the genesis of such beings. In grasping to understand, we project the viewer outwards. All one is able to perceive is themselves. Why do we fear the reflection?

Fingers twitch still, evoking a spider more than a human. Veins of umber run through pallid flesh leading to talons tipping the eight digits, a sickening magnolia. The wrist is all torn sinew. Halve the Constitution of the wearer. When the hand is used in a task, double the Strength of the wearer. Once per day, the wearer may strike at the air, opening a portal as Dimension Door. Any gripped by the fist are subject to Dispel Magic.

4. Stolen Shroud

To slip between the barrier of life and death, one must first confuse the living. A used funeral shroud, the donor body still in the ground. This complete, they crumble, merging with the rubble they have made.

Any instructional material relating to these orbs, found in buried ruins and flooded cities, details commands for the construction of buildings. Using any such commands causes the being to enter a killing frenzy. Their long wait has turned them hostile to the concept of building.
